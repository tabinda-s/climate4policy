    Climate Change in California

    Climate change has become somewhat of a taboo subject in America and yet continues to progress while we are at a political standstill. Last year, America’s carbon dioxide emissions rose by 3.4% in the second largest gain since 1996. As the second largest greenhouse gas emitter in the world, the United States plays a major role in making or breaking the global commitment in the Paris Agreement (to which, contrary to popular belief, the United States is still a party to keep warming within 2 degrees celsius above pre-industrial levels. Already, oceans are warming faster than expected and many scientists worry that we are on track for irreversible tipping points if we do not abate emissions quickly.

    These statements can feel big, abstract, and intractable to many citizens and yet, many climate change policy experts acknowledge that it is solvable . We have chosen to show, through the case of California, that it is in the best interest of the country to make major investments in climate change mitigation and adaptation and that, by doing so, we need not undermine the economy. Property damages from fires and sea level rises as well as public health risks from extreme temperatures make climate change too costly to ignore even if we do not consider global costs. Below we first will show how California has changed over the recent years and then discuss climate-induced hazards and projected costs. We will conclude by proposing an alternate way forward.

    The extreme heat map perhaps best exposed the substantial heterogeneity across California. Inland and southern counties experienced much hotter maximum monthly temperatures, while some counties around the bay area actually saw their hottest temperatures decrease. Following the Mark Twain quote “The coldest winter I ever spent was a summer in San Francisco”, one reason for this surprising trend could be due to fog. The Bay Area receives a lot of fog during high temperatures which is a natural safeguard against high temperatures.     Climate change can increase wildfire risk in California through its effects on moisture availability, temperature, vector prevalence, and lightning. Several academic papers have linked climate effects by showing that climate change has increased drought in California, led to more pine beetle infestations and tree mortality, and augmented the flammability of vegetation that fuels wildfires. As a result, modelling efforts predict that wildfires will become more frequent and larger throughout the century. In addition, separate research has shown lightning strikes in the western United States are will become more frequent in an altered climate, which bodes poorly for California where wildfires are most often ignited by storms. With higher temperatures, fires are expected to rage hotter and lay scourge to more area. For these reasons, normally-cautious scientists have begun to draw a causal link between climate change and forest fire. With large fires in the state over the past few years, the media has joined the frenzy, proclaiming that California is burning and climate change is the cause. Above, we showed that California has gotten dryer and hotter especially in the areas of the Mountains where many of the state’s forests lie.     The Cost of Wildfires in California

    Wildfires cost the state of California an upward of 3 billion dollars in the year 2015 - a figure that has likely increased in 2018 after the deadly fires in Paradise and Malibu. In 1990, wildfires in California cost $95,707,749 and while it can be argued that inflation over time make the figure in billions look much more intimidating than is true, it is still quite a hefty sum to be dealing with -- one that California’s residents pay for. California’s population of 39 million people contributes about 76 dollars per person to fight wildfires.     The state of California has begun taking steps to address these challenges through more aggressive regulation and policies that promote investment in mitigation and adaptation. Skeptics argue that this environment is unduly burdensome for businesses and taxpayers who must compete with areas with looser regulations. Historical patterns of development have shown a direct relationship between economic growth and greenhouse gas emissions -- as economic output grows, greenhouse gas emissions grow in tandem. However, this relationship is not set in stone.

    Recent trends show increasing emissions are not requisite for driving growth.

    The data show that many states have successfully decoupled economic growth from greenhouse gas emissions. As GDP per capita increases, carbon dioxide emissions flatten or even decrease. This is true even in more heavily regulated states like California, showing that growth and regulation are not diametrically opposed.

    Adaptation and mitigation for the future can successfully coexist with growth in the present. Proactively combatting the effects of climate change is the smart thing to do -- both today and tomorrow.
