Hazards: Wildfire

Climate change can increase wildfire risk in California through its effects on moisture availability, temperature, vector prevalence, and lightning. Several academic papers have linked climate effects by showing that climate change has increased drought in California, led to more pine beetle infestations and tree mortality, and augmented the flammability of vegetation that fuels wildfires. As a result, modelling efforts predict that wildfires will become more frequent and larger throughout the century. In addition, separate research has shown lightning strikes in the western United States are will become more frequent in an altered climate, which bodes poorly for California where wildfires are most often ignited by storms. With higher temperatures, fires are expected to rage hotter and lay scourge to more area. For these reasons, normally-cautious scientists have begun to draw a causal link between climate change and forest fire. With large fires in the state over the past few years, the media has joined the frenzy, proclaiming that California is burning and climate change is the cause. Above, we showed that California has gotten dryer and hotter especially in the areas of the Mountains where many of the state’s forests lie. Below we have taken a separate data source to show how those changes have corresponded to fire trends. Acres burned and structures damaged due to wildfires have witnessed a sharp increase. A look at the data from 1990 - 2018 shows that in 1991 2,900 acres were burned while that number rose to 479, 936 in the last year. Similarly, Similarly, in 2018 nearly 22,000 structures were damaged compared to 636 structures in 1992. Wildfires cost the state of California an upward of 3 billion dollars in the year 2015 - a figure that has likely increased in 2018 after the deadly fires in Paradise and Malibu. In 1990, wildfires in California cost $95,707,749 and while it can be argued that inflation over time make the figure in billions look much more intimidating than is true, it is still quite a hefty sum to be dealing with -- one that California’s residents pay for. California’s population of 39 million people contributes about 76 dollars per person to fight wildfires.
